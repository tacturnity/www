Chapter 15: Machines

15.3 Rotating-Coil Devices (Pages 969-980)

Transducers using rotation.

The d'Arsonval Movement (Fig. 15.15, Fig. 15.16)

Core of analog DC meters.

  • Structure:
    • Stationary permanent magnet creating a radial magnetic field in an air gap.
    • Stationary iron core within the gap to ensure uniform radial field.
    • Rectangular N-turn coil, free to rotate on jeweled bearings around the iron core.
    • Two springs: provide restraining torque and electrical connection to the coil.
    • Pointer attached to coil assembly indicates angular deflection $\gamma$ on a scale.
  • Operation (Fig. 15.16 - top view of air gap):
    • Current $i$ through the coil. Coil sides (length $l$) are perpendicular to $B$.
    • Force on one side: $f_{side} = Bli$.
    • Torque from one side: $T_{side} = f_{side} \cdot r = Blir$ (where $r$ is distance from axis to conductor).
    • Total torque on N-turn coil (2 sides active):
$$ T_{coil} = 2NBlri \quad (Eq. 15.18) $$

clockwise.

  • Restraining Torque from Springs:
$$ T_{springs} = \frac{\gamma}{K} \quad (Eq. 15.19) $$

counterclockwise. ($K$ is rotational compliance of springs).

  • Equilibrium: $T_{coil} = T_{springs}$. $2NBlri = \gamma / K$. Deflection:
$$ \gamma = (2KNBlr)i \quad (Eq. 15.20) $$

Deflection $\gamma$ is directly proportional to current $i$. -> Linear scale for DC ammeters/voltmeters.

Example 15.5: d'Arsonval Movement Calculation

  • Coil: $l = 2\text{cm} = 0.02\text{m}$, $r = 1\text{cm} = 0.01\text{m}$. Field $B = 0.5\text{T}$. Spring compliance $K = 2 \times 10^8 \text{ degrees/(N}\cdot\text{m)}$. Full-scale deflection $\gamma_{fs} = 90 \text{ degrees}$ for current $i_{fs} = 50 \text{ µA} = 50 \times 10^{-6} \text{ A}$. Find $N$.
  • From Eq. 15.20: $N = \gamma / (2KB l r i)$.
  • $N = 90 / (2 \cdot (2 \times 10^8) \cdot 0.5 \cdot 0.02 \cdot 0.01 \cdot (50 \times 10^{-6})) = 90 / 2 = 45 \text{ turns}$.

Ammeters (Fig. 15.17)

Using d'Arsonval movement to measure current.

  • The basic movement measures small currents (e.g., 0-50 µA).
  • To measure larger currents, a shunt resistor $R_p$ is placed in parallel with the movement (coil resistance $R_c$, inductance $L_c$).
  • For DC, $L_c$ is a short. Current $i_c$ through coil by current division: $i_c = (R_p / (R_p + R_c)) \cdot i_{total}$.
  • Solving for $R_p$:
$$ R_p = \frac{i_c}{(i_{total} - i_c)} R_c \quad (Eq. 15.21) $$

Example 15.6: Ammeter Shunt

  • Movement: 50 µA full scale, $R_c = 2 \text{ k}\Omega$. Desired range: 0-100 mA.
  • $i_{c\_fs} = 50 \text{ µA}$, $i_{total\_fs} = 100 \text{ mA} = 100,000 \text{ µA}$.
  • $R_p = (50 \text{ µA} / (100,000 \text{ µA} - 50 \text{ µA})) \cdot 2000 \Omega \approx (50 / 100,000) \cdot 2000 \Omega = 1.00 \Omega$.
  • Ideal ammeter has zero resistance. Low shunt resistance makes the overall ammeter resistance low.

Voltmeters (Fig. 15.18)

Using d'Arsonval movement to measure DC voltage.

  • A series resistor (multiplier resistor) $R_s$ is connected in series with the movement.
  • For full-scale voltage $v_{fs}$, current through movement is $i_{fs}$.
  • By Ohm's Law: $v_{fs} = i_{fs} \cdot (R_s + R_c)$.
  • Solving for $R_s$: $R_s = (v_{fs} / i_{fs}) - R_c$.

Example 15.7: Voltmeter Multiplier

  • Movement: 50 µA full scale, $R_c = 2 \text{ k}\Omega$. Desired range: 0-20 V.
  • $i_{fs} = 50 \text{ µA}$.
  • $R_s = (20 \text{ V} / (50 \times 10^{-6} \text{ A})) - 2000 \Omega = 400,000 \Omega - 2000 \Omega = 398,000 \Omega = 398 \text{ k}\Omega$.
  • Total voltmeter resistance: $R_s + R_c = 398 \text{ k}\Omega + 2 \text{ k}\Omega = 400 \text{ k}\Omega$.
  • Ohms per volt rating: Total resistance / full-scale voltage. $400,000 \Omega / 20 \text{ V} = 20,000 \Omega/\text{V} = 20 \text{ k}\Omega/\text{V}$.
  • Ideal voltmeter has infinite resistance. High total resistance is desirable.

Ohmmeters (Fig. 15.19)

Using d'Arsonval movement to measure resistance.

  • Basic Setup: Movement (coil $R_c$), series resistor $R_s$, and a battery $V_s$ are all in series. The unknown resistance $R_x$ is connected across the terminals.
  • Current $i$ through movement: $i = V_s / (R_s + R_c + R_x)$.
  • Calibration:
    • Full-scale deflection ($i_{fs}$): When $R_x = 0$ (terminals shorted). $R_s$ is chosen so $i_{fs} = V_s / (R_s + R_c)$.
    • Zero deflection ($i=0$): When $R_x = \infty$ (terminals open).
    • Other points: e.g., half-scale deflection when $R_x = R_s + R_c$.
  • Scale is nonlinear and reads high resistance on left, low on right.

Example 15.8: Ohmmeter Design

  • Movement: 50 µA fs, $R_c = 2 \text{ k}\Omega$. Battery $V_s = 1.5 \text{ V}$.
  • Find $R_s$ for fs deflection when $R_x = 0$: $50 \times 10^{-6} \text{ A} = 1.5 \text{ V} / (R_s + 2000 \Omega)$ $R_s + 2000 = 1.5 / (50 \times 10^{-6}) = 30,000 \Omega$. So $R_s = 28 \text{ k}\Omega$.
  • Resistance for half-scale deflection ($i = 25 \text{ µA}$): $R_x = (V_s / i) - R_c - R_s = (1.5 / (25 \times 10^{-6})) - 2000 - 28000 = 60000 - 30000 = 30 \text{ k}\Omega$.
  • Multimeter (VOM): A single d'Arsonval movement with switches to select different shunt/series resistors for various current, voltage, and resistance ranges.

The Electrodynamometer (Fig. 15.20)

Rotational transducer, useful for AC measurements.

  • Structure:
    • No permanent magnet. Magnetic field $B$ is supplied by a stationary coil (solenoid), split into two sections. Core is nonmagnetic (avoids eddy/hysteresis losses). $B$ is proportional to current $i_s$ in stationary coil: $B = K_s i_s$.
    • Movable N-turn coil (similar to d'Arsonval) restrained by springs, with a pointer.
  • Operation: Current $i_c$ in movable coil.
    • If plane of moving coil is at angle $\psi$ with direction of $B$, effective distance for torque $d = r \cos \psi$.
    • Torque on movable coil: $T = 2NBl i_c r \cos \psi = 2NB i_c A_c \cos \psi / 2 = N B i_c A_c \cos \psi$ (where $A_c = 2lr$ is area of coil). The text uses $T = 2NBl i_c (r \cos \psi)$ based on $l$ as length and $2r$ as width. $T = 2NK_s l r \cos(\psi) \cdot i_s \cdot i_c$.
  • Restraining Torque: $T_0 = \gamma / K$.
  • Equilibrium Deflection $\gamma$:
$$ \gamma = (2NKK_s l r \cos \psi) i_c i_s \quad (Eq. 15.23) $$

Deflection depends on the product $i_c i_s$.

  • AC Measurements:
    • If $i_c = I_c \cos(\omega t + \theta_1)$ and $i_s = I_s \cos(\omega t + \theta_2)$.
    • Mechanical inertia averages the deflection. Average of $i_c i_s$ is $(1/2) I_c I_s \cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2)$. (The text simplifies to $I_c I_s \cos \theta$).
    • Average deflection:
$$ \gamma_{avg} = K_\\psi I_c I_s \cos \\theta \quad (Eq. 15.24) $$

where $\theta = \theta_1 - \theta_2$. ($K_\psi$ includes geometric factors and $\cos \psi$).

  • AC Ammeter/Voltmeter: If stationary and moving coils are in series, $i_c = i_s = i = I \cos(\omega t)$. Then $\theta = 0$, $\cos \theta = 1$. $\gamma_{avg}$ is proportional to $I_c I_s = I^2$. Scale can be graduated to read RMS current ($I / \sqrt{2}$). More common for AC voltmeters than ammeters.

The Wattmeter (Fig. 15.21)

Most important use of electrodynamometer. Measures average power.

  • Connection:
    • Fixed coil (current coil): Carries load current $i_s = i_{load}$. Placed in series with the load. Low resistance.
    • Moving coil (voltage coil/potential coil): Connected in series with a large resistor $R_s$. This combination is placed in parallel with the load. Current $i_c$ is proportional to load voltage $v_{load}$: $i_c \approx v_{load} / R_s$. High resistance.
  • Operation:
    • Deflection $\gamma$ is proportional to $I_c I_s \cos \theta$.
    • Here, $I_s$ is RMS load current $I$. $I_c$ is proportional to RMS load voltage $V$ ($I_c = V/R_s$).
    • $\theta$ is the phase angle between load voltage and load current.
    • So, deflection $\gamma$ is proportional to $(V/R_s) \cdot I \cdot \cos \theta = (1/R_s) \cdot P_{avg}$.
    • Scale graduated to read average power $P_{avg} = VI \cos \theta$.
  • Polarity Marks (±): Ensure upscale deflection. If current enters (or leaves) ± mark on both coils simultaneously, deflection is upscale.
  • Accuracy Note: Wattmeter reading $P_W$ includes power loss in the voltage coil if connected as in Fig 15.21 (voltage coil across load + current coil). If $P_L$ is true load power: $P_L = P_W - V_L^2 / (R_s + R_{c\_moving})$. Or more simply $P_L = P_W - V_L^2/R_s$ if $R_s$ dominates voltage coil impedance.
    • Alternatively, if voltage coil is connected directly across load, $P_W$ includes power loss in current coil: $P_L = P_W - I_s^2 R_{c\_fixed}$.
  • Compensated Wattmeters: Have an additional coil to cancel the effect of $i_c$ in the current coil circuit for more accurate $P_L$ reading.

15.4 DC Machines (Pages 980-1001)

Focus on rotating machines.

Rotating Machines - Basic Principles (Fig. 15.22, 15.23)

  • Simple AC Generator (Alternator) (Fig. 15.22a):
    • Rectangular loop (length $l$, width $2r$, Area $A = 2rl$) rotates at angular velocity $\omega$ in uniform magnetic field $B$.
    • Angle between plane of loop and y-z plane is $\omega t$. Flux through loop $\Phi = BA \cos(\omega t)$.
    • Slip Rings: Ends of loop connected to slip rings (rotate with loop).
    • Brushes: Fixed, make contact with slip rings.
    • Induced EMF (Faraday's Law): $e = -d\Phi/dt = -d(BA \cos \omega t)/dt = BA\omega \sin \omega t$. (Eq. 15.25 with minus sign from Lenz's Law and specific geometry). If N-turns:
$$ e = -NBA\\omega \sin \\omega t \quad (Eq. 15.26) $$
  • This is an AC voltage.
  • Simple DC Generator (Fig. 15.23a):
    • Modify alternator: replace slip rings with a split-ring commutator.
    • Brushes repositioned.
    • Commutator acts as a mechanical rectifier. Voltage $v_{12}$ across loop is $e = -BA\omega \sin \omega t$.
    • Brush voltage $v$:
      • For $0 < \omega t < \pi$, $\sin \omega t > 0$, $e$ is negative. If brushes arranged appropriately, $v = -e = BA\omega \sin \omega t$.
      • For $\pi < \omega t < 2\pi$, $\sin \omega t < 0$, $e$ is positive. Commutator switches connection, so $v = e = -BA\omega \sin \omega t$ (which is positive in this range).
    • Result: $v = |BA\omega \sin \omega t|$ (Fig. 15.24b). A pulsating DC voltage (rectified sine wave).

DC Generators

  • Stator (Field): Stationary part. Produces magnetic field via direct current in field windings on poles.
  • Rotor (Armature): Rotating part. Contains conductors where EMFs are induced, and the commutator.
  • Simplified DC Generator (Fig. 15.25):
    • 2-pole stator shown (can be more, always even).
    • Rotor: cylindrical iron core with slots for armature conductors, commutator segments.
    • Brushes make contact with commutator.
  • Coil Connections (Fig. 15.26): Multiple coils connected to commutator segments.
    • EMF in a single coil is rectified (Fig. 15.27).
    • Commutator connects coils in series between brushes such that their rectified EMFs add up.
    • Output voltage across brushes (Fig. 15.28) is smoother DC with small commutator ripple.

Generated Voltage ($v_g$)

  • $p$ = number of poles.
  • $\Phi$ = flux per pole (Wb).
  • $n$ = rotor speed (rpm).
  • $N$ = total number of armature conductors.
  • $a$ = number of parallel paths in armature winding between brushes.
  • Average EMF per conductor = $\Phi \cdot (p \cdot n/60)$ (flux cut per second).
  • Number of conductors in series per path = $N/a$.
  • Total generated voltage (no-load):
$$ v_g = \frac{Np\\Phi n}{60a} \quad (Eq. 15.27) $$

Example 15.9: DC Generator Voltage

  • $p=2$, $a=2$. Pole area $A = 0.01 \text{ m}^2$, $B = 1 \text{ T}$. 4 armature coils, 12 turns/coil.
  • Flux per pole $\Phi = BA = 1 \cdot 0.01 = 0.01 \text{ Wb}$.
  • Total conductors $N = (4 \text{ coils}) \cdot (12 \text{ turns/coil}) \cdot (2 \text{ conductors/turn}) = 96$.
  • Speed $n = 1500 \text{ rpm}$.
  • $v_g = (96 \cdot 2 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 1500) / (60 \cdot 2) = (96 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 1500) / 60 = 24 \text{ V}$.

Generator with a Load (Fig. 15.29)

  • Armature has resistance $R_a$ (windings, brush contacts).
  • When loaded, terminal voltage $v_L$ is less than $v_g$.
  • $v_L = v_g - I_a R_a$ (where $I_a$ is armature current, same as load current $I_L$ for separate excitation).

Example 15.10: Loaded DC Generator

  • 8kW, 200V DC generator, full-load current $I_L = 40\text{A}$ at $n_1 = 1200 \text{ rpm}$. $R_a = 0.5 \Omega$.
  • At 1200 rpm, full load: $v_{g1} = v_{L1} + I_L R_a = 200\text{V} + 40\text{A} \cdot 0.5\Omega = 200 + 20 = 220\text{V}$. This is no-load voltage at 1200 rpm.
  • $v_g$ is proportional to speed $n$ (if $\Phi$ constant).
  • At $n_2 = 900 \text{ rpm}$: $v_{g2} = v_{g1} \cdot (n_2 / n_1) = 220\text{V} \cdot (900 / 1200) = 220\text{V} \cdot (3/4) = 165\text{V}$. This is no-load voltage at 900 rpm.
  • Full-load voltage at 900 rpm (assuming $I_L = 40\text{A}$ is still full load capacity): $v_{L2} = v_{g2} - I_L R_a = 165\text{V} - 40\text{A} \cdot 0.5\Omega = 165 - 20 = 145\text{V}$.

Generator Symbol (Fig. 15.30)

Separate symbols for field ($i_f$) and armature ($i_a$). Prime mover (engine/turbine) is implicit.

Magnetization Curves (Fig. 15.31)

$$ v_g = K\\Phi n \quad (Eq. 15.28) $$

where $K = Np/(60a)$.

  • Flux $\Phi$ depends on field current $i_f$. Relationship is nonlinear (like B-H curve for magnetic material) due to saturation.
  • Plot of no-load $v_g$ vs. $i_f$ at constant speed $n$ is a magnetization curve.
  • Curves for different speeds are scaled versions (proportional to $n$).
  • Due to residual magnetism, $v_g$ is not zero when $i_f = 0$.

Generator Field Excitation

Ways to supply DC to field winding ($R_f$ is implicit field resistance).

  1. Separately Excited Generator (Fig. 15.32): Field winding connected to an external DC source $V_f$.
  2. Self-Excited Generator: Uses its own generated voltage.
    • Shunt-Connected (Fig. 15.33a): Field winding in parallel (shunt) with armature. Field winding has many turns, high resistance $R_f$. $i_f$ is small. Control Rheostat $R_c$ (Fig. 15.33b) in series with field to adjust $i_f$ and thus $v_g$.
    • Series-Connected (Fig. 15.34a): Field winding in series with armature. $i_f = i_a$. Field winding has few turns, low resistance. Diverter Resistance $R_d$ (Fig. 15.34b) in parallel with series field to divert some $i_a$. Rarely used.
    • Compound-Connected (Fig. 15.35a): Has both shunt and series field windings. Can use control rheostat and diverter (Fig. 15.35b).
      • Cumulative Compounding: Series field mmf aids shunt field mmf (typical).
      • Differential Compounding: Series field mmf opposes shunt field mmf.

Generator Buildup (Shunt Generator, Fig. 15.36, 15.37)

How a self-excited shunt generator starts generating voltage.

  1. Residual magnetism produces small initial $v_g$ when rotor turns.
  2. If field switch $S$ is closed, this small $v_g$ (across armature) is applied to field circuit ($R_f + R_c$).
  3. This causes small $i_f$. If $i_f$ aids residual flux, $\Phi$ increases.
  4. Increased $\Phi$ -> increased $v_g$. Increased $v_g$ -> increased $i_f$.
  5. Process continues until stable operating point is reached.
  • Operating Point: Intersection of magnetization curve ($v_g$ vs $i_f$) and field resistance line ($v_g = i_f \cdot R_{total\_field}$, where $R_{total\_field} = R_f + R_c$). (Fig. 15.37).
    • For stable operation, field resistance line should intersect the nonlinear (saturated) part of magnetization curve.
    • If $R_{total\_field}$ is too high (line too steep), intersection is very low, little buildup ("critical field resistance").

External Characteristics (Load Voltage $v_L$ vs. Load Current $i_L$)

  1. Separately Excited Generator (Fig. 15.38):
$$ v_L = v_g - I_a R_a \quad (Eq. 15.29) $$

If $v_g$ (due to constant $i_f$) is constant, $v_L$ drops linearly with $I_L$.

  • Actually slightly curved down due to armature reaction (armature current's magnetic field distorts main field, slightly reducing net flux and $v_g$).
  1. Shunt-Connected Generator (Fig. 15.38):
    • $v_L$ drops more steeply than separately excited.
    • As $I_L$ increases, $v_L$ drops due to $I_a R_a$.
    • This lower $v_L$ is also the voltage across shunt field, so $i_f$ decreases.
    • Decreased $i_f$ -> decreased $\Phi$ -> decreased $v_g$. This further drops $v_L$.
  2. Compound-Connected Generator (Fig. 15.39):
    • Cumulative Compounding: Series field aids shunt field. As $I_L$ (and thus series $i_f$) increases, flux from series field boosts total flux.
      • Flat-Compounded: Series field compensates $R_a$ drop and armature reaction, so $v_L$ at full load $\approx v_L$ at no load.
      • Overcompounded: $v_L$ at full load > $v_L$ at no load. Series field effect is strong.
      • Undercompounded: $v_L$ drops, but less than shunt. Series field effect is weak.
  • Armature Reaction: MMF from armature current distorts main pole flux. Can affect commutation.
    • Compensating Windings (Pole-Face Windings): In slots on pole faces, series with armature. Produce MMF opposing armature MMF, reducing distortion.
    • Commutating Poles (Interpoles): Narrow poles between main poles, series with armature. Produce field to aid current reversal in coils undergoing commutation, reducing sparking at brushes.

DC Motors (Pages 994-1001)

Basically same construction as DC generators.

Simple Rotating Motor (Fig. 15.40)

  • Loop in $B$ field. Current $i$ applied.
  • Force on upper conductor $f = Bli$ (downwards). Force on lower $f = Bli$ (upwards).
  • Torque on each conductor $fd = Blir \sin \delta$ ($\delta$ is torque/power angle between plane of loop and y-z plane).
  • Total torque for N-turn coil (2N conductors):
$$ T = 2NBlir \\sin \\delta = NBAi \\sin \\delta \quad (Eq. 15.30) $$

Max when $\delta=90^\circ$.

  • This torque rotates loop.
  • As loop rotates, it also generates a back EMF (counter EMF) that opposes applied current.

Armature Model for a DC Motor (Fig. 15.41)

  • Field current direction and rotation direction usually same as generator. Armature current direction is reversed.
  • Applied line voltage $v_L$. Armature resistance $R_a$. Back EMF $v_g = K\Phi n$.
  • KVL:
$$ v_L = I_a R_a + v_g \quad (Eq. 15.31) $$

($I_a$ direction into armature $+$ terminal).

  • So,
$$ v_L = I_a R_a + K\\Phi n \quad (Eq. 15.32) $$
  • Speed Equation:
$$ n = \frac{(v_L - I_a R_a)}{K\\Phi} \quad (Eq. 15.33) $$
  • Developed Torque Equation:
$$ T = K\'\\Phi I_a \quad (Eq. 15.34) $$

($K'$ is another constant).

DC Shunt Motors (Fig. 15.42)

Field winding in shunt with armature, across $v_L$.

  • If $v_L$ is constant, and field rheostat $R_c$ is fixed, then $i_f$ is constant, so $\Phi$ is relatively constant (ignoring armature reaction).
  • Since $I_a R_a$ is usually small compared to $v_L$, $n \approx v_L / (K\Phi)$. Thus, a shunt motor is nearly a constant speed motor.
  • Speed control: Vary $R_c$ (changes $i_f \rightarrow \Phi$) or vary $v_L$ (if possible).
  • Speed-Torque Characteristic (Fig. 15.43):
    • From speed and torque equations: $n = (v_L / K\Phi) - (R_a / (KK\'\Phi^2)) \cdot T$.
    • This is $n = b - mT$. A straight line with negative slope (actually slightly curved due to armature reaction). Speed drops slightly as torque (load) increases.
    • $n_r, T_r$ are rated speed and torque at full load.

Example 15.11: Shunt Motor Speed

  • $v_L = 220\text{V}$. At no load: $n_{NL} = 1200 \text{ rpm}$, $I_{aNL} = 6\text{A}$. $R_a = 0.5\Omega$.
  • Find speed $n_{FL}$ when $I_{aFL} = 40\text{A}$ (full load). Assume $\Phi$ is constant.
  • From $n = (v_L - I_a R_a) / K\Phi$, so $K\Phi = (v_L - I_a R_a) / n$.
  • At no load: $K\Phi = (220 - 6 \cdot 0.5) / 1200 = (220 - 3) / 1200 = 217 / 1200 \approx 0.1808$.
  • At full load: $n_{FL} = (v_L - I_{aFL} R_a) / K\Phi = (220 - 40 \cdot 0.5) / 0.1808 = (220 - 20) / 0.1808 \approx 1106 \text{ rpm}$. (Text has 0.181, giving 1105 rpm).
  • Speed Regulation (SR): Measure of speed change from no load to full load. $SR = (n_{NL} - n_{FL}) / n_{FL}$. For example: $(1200 - 1105) / 1105 = 95 / 1105 \approx 0.086 = 8.6\%$. Typical shunt motor SR: 5-12%.

DC Series Motors (Fig. 15.44)

Field winding in series with armature. $i_f = i_a$. Field has few turns, heavy wire.

  • Flux $\Phi$ depends on $i_a$. Assume linear magnetization: $\Phi = K_f i_f = K_f i_a$.
  • Back EMF
$$ v_g = K\\Phi n = K(K_f i_a)n = KK_f i_a n \quad (Eq. 15.35) $$
  • Torque
$$ T = K\'\\Phi i_a = K\'(K_f i_a)i_a = K\'K_f i_a^2 \quad (Eq. 15.36) $$

Torque proportional to $i_a^2$.

  • Let $R = R_a + R_f$ (total series resistance). $v_L = I_a R + v_g = I_a R + KK_f I_a n = I_a (R + KK_f n)$. So $I_a = v_L / (R + KK_f n)$.
  • Substitute $I_a$ into torque equation:
$$ T = K\'K_f \left( \frac{v_L}{R + KK_f n} \right)^2 \quad (Eq. 15.37) $$
  • Speed-Torque Characteristic (Fig. 15.45):
    • Speed $n$ increases rapidly as torque $T$ (load) decreases.
    • Dangerously high speed at no load. Series motors must always be mechanically coupled to a load.
    • Used for variable-speed, high starting torque applications (cranes, hoists, electric drills).
  • Universal Motors: Since torque direction is same regardless of current direction ($T \sim i_a^2$), series motors can run on AC or DC. These are called universal motors. Torque pulsates on AC. Common in hand tools, appliances.

DC Compound Motors

Have both shunt and series fields. Usually cumulative.

  • Speed-Torque Characteristic (Fig. 15.46): Lies between shunt and series motor characteristics.

Starting DC Motors

  • At startup, $n=0$, so back EMF $v_g = K\Phi n = 0$.
  • From $v_L = I_a R_a + v_g$, if $v_g=0$, then $I_a = v_L / R_a$.
  • Since $R_a$ is small, initial $I_a$ can be excessively large.
  • Starting Box: A variable resistance placed in series with armature at startup. Limits $I_{a\_start}$ to 150-200% of rated. Resistance is gradually removed as motor speeds up and $v_g$ builds.

15.5 AC Machines (Pages 1001-1026)

The Alternator (Synchronous Generator) (Fig. 15.47)

Typically, field (rotor) rotates, armature (stator) is stationary.

  • Reason: Armature handles more power; easier to take high power from stationary windings. Field DC supplied via slip rings/brushes to rotor.
  • Structure (Fig. 15.47 - simplified 2-pole):
    • Rotor: Field winding. DC current creates N and S poles.
    • Stator: Armature conductors (a-a', b-b', c-c') embedded in slots, forming coils.
    • As rotor field rotates, changing flux induces sinusoidal EMFs in stator coils.
    • Coils a-a', b-b', c-c' are physically spaced $120^\circ$ apart. EMFs induced are $120^\circ$ phase-shifted (Fig. 15.48).
    • Connecting coils in Wye (Y) or Delta ($\Delta$) produces a three-phase voltage source.
  • Frequency: If rotor speed is $n$ rpm (n/60 rps), for a 2-pole machine, one revolution = one cycle of EMF. Electrical frequency $f_e = n/60$ Hz. Synchronized with mechanical speed $n$. -> Synchronous Machine.
  • Multi-pole Alternators (Fig. 15.49 - 4-pole):
    • For $p$ poles (even number), one revolution produces $p/2$ cycles of EMF.
    • Frequency
$$ f_e = \frac{p}{2} \cdot \frac{n}{60} = \frac{pn}{120} \quad (Eq. 15.38) $$

Hz.

  • E.g., for $f_e = 60 \text{ Hz}$:
    • 2-pole: $n = 3600 \text{ rpm}$.
    • 4-pole: $n = 1800 \text{ rpm}$.
  • Rotor Types:
    • Salient-Pole: Prominent poles (Fig 15.47, 15.49). For lower speeds (e.g., hydro-generators).
    • Cylindrical (Nonsalient) Rotor: Field winding distributed in slots. For high speeds (e.g., turboalternators, 1800/3600 rpm) to reduce mechanical stress and windage.

Alternator Armature Model (Single Phase) (Fig. 15.50)

  • Per-phase equivalent circuit.
  • Generated EMF $V_g$ (phasor).
  • Armature resistance $R_a$.
  • Synchronous Reactance $X_s$: Represents effects of armature winding inductance and armature reaction (effect of stator MMF on rotor field).
  • Terminal voltage $V_L$ (phasor). Armature current $I_a$ (phasor).
  • KVL: $V_g = V_L + I_a R_a + j I_a X_s$. (Or $V_L = V_g - I_a(R_a + jX_s)$ if $I_a$ flows out of $V_g$).
  • $R_a$ is often much smaller than $X_s$ and ignored.
  • $V_g$ (RMS magnitude) related to field current $i_f$ and speed $n$ by a magnetization curve (AC voltages, so RMS on vertical axis).

Example 15.12: Alternator Calculation

  • 6-pole, 3-phase, 12 kVA, Y-connected. Rated per-phase $V_L = 125\text{V}$ (RMS) at load pf = 0.6 lagging, 60 Hz. $X_s = 2\Omega$, $R_a \approx 0$.
  • Rotor Speed (n): From $f = pn/120 \Rightarrow n = 120f/p = 120 \cdot 60/6 = 1200 \text{ rpm}$.
  • Armature Current ($I_a$): Per-phase power = 12 kVA / 3 = 4 kVA. $I_a = (\text{Per-phase VA}) / V_L = 4000 \text{ VA} / 125 \text{ V} = 32 \text{ A}$.
  • Phase Angle: $\text{pf} = 0.6$ lagging $\Rightarrow \theta = \cos^{-1}(0.6) = 53.1^\circ$. If $V_L$ is reference: $V_L = 125\angle 0^\circ \text{ V}$. Then $I_a = 32\angle -53.1^\circ \text{ A}$.
  • Generated Voltage ($V_g$): (Model from Fig 15.50, KVL for alternator: $V_g = V_L + I_a(jX_s)$ assuming $R_a=0$). $V_g = 125\angle 0^\circ + (32\angle -53.1^\circ)(j2) = 125\angle 0^\circ + (32\angle -53.1^\circ)(2\angle 90^\circ)$. $V_g = 125 + 64\angle (90 - 53.1)^\circ = 125 + 64\angle 36.9^\circ$. $V_g = 125 + (51.2 + j38.4) = 176.2 + j38.4 = 180.3\angle 12.3^\circ \text{ V}$. Magnitude $V_g \approx 180 \text{ V}$.
  • Phasor diagram (Fig. 15.52): $V_g$ leads $V_L$.

The Rotating Stator Field (Fig. 15.53)

Essential for AC motors.

  • Consider 3 stator coils (a-a', b-b', c-c') spaced $120^\circ$ physically.
  • Fed by balanced 3-phase currents: $i_a = I \cos(\omega t)$ $i_b = I \cos(\omega t - 120^\circ)$ $i_c = I \cos(\omega t - 240^\circ) = I \cos(\omega t + 120^\circ)$. (Fig. 15.54)
  • Each current produces a pulsating flux aligned with its coil axis ($\Phi_a, \Phi_b, \Phi_c$). Assume flux $\phi = ki$.
  • Total flux $\Phi_{total} = \Phi_a + \Phi_b + \Phi_c$ (vector sum).
  • Analysis using complex numbers (flux $\Phi e^{j\theta}$): $\Phi_{a\_complex} = (kI \cos \omega t) e^{j0^\circ}$ $\Phi_{b\_complex} = (kI \cos(\omega t-120^\circ)) e^{-j120^\circ}$ (or $e^{j120^\circ}$ if axes defined differently, text shows b lagging a) $\Phi_{c\_complex} = (kI \cos(\omega t+120^\circ)) e^{j120^\circ}$ (or $e^{-j240^\circ}$) The sum (after trigonometric expansion and simplification, see page 1007-1008): $\Phi_{total\_complex} = (3/2)kI e^{-j\omega t}$. (Or $e^{j\omega t}$ depending on rotation sense assumed).
  • Result: The total stator flux has a constant magnitude $(3/2)kI$ and rotates at angular velocity $\omega$ (synchronous speed).

The Synchronous Motor (Fig. 15.55)

Construction like alternator.

  • Stator: 3-phase winding creates rotating magnetic field (at $\omega_s$).
  • Rotor: DC field winding creates fixed N-S poles (relative to rotor).
  • Operation:
    • Magnetic attraction between rotating stator field poles and DC rotor poles.
    • Rotor "locks in" and rotates at the same speed as stator field (synchronous speed $n_s$).
    • Torque Angle ($\delta$) (Fig. 15.55): Angle between stator field axis and rotor field axis.
      • No load: $\delta$ small (overcomes friction/windage).
      • Load applied: $\delta$ increases. Rotor field lags stator field.
      • If load torque > max developed torque, motor loses synchronism and stops.
  • Armature Model (Fig. 15.56): Same as alternator, but $I_a$ direction reversed (power flows into motor).
    • KVL: $V_L = I_a R_a + j I_a X_s + V_g$. (Or $V_L = I_a(R_a + jX_s) + V_g$).
    • Ignoring $R_a$:
$$ V_L = j I_a X_s + V_g \quad (Eq. 15.39) $$
  • $V_g$ (generated/back EMF due to rotor field) lags $V_L$ by $\delta$.
  • Power Equation (Fig. 15.57, 15.58 - phasor diagrams):
    • From geometry of phasor diagram (assuming $R_a=0$), can derive
$$ V_g \\sin \\delta = X_s I_a \\cos \\theta \quad (Eq. 15.40) $$

where $\theta$ is power factor angle between $V_L$ and $I_a$.

  • Input power per phase $P_{in} = V_L I_a \cos \theta$.
  • Substitute $I_a \cos \theta$:
$$ P_{in} = \frac{V_L V_g}{X_s} \\sin \\delta \quad (Eq. 15.41) $$
  • $\delta$ is also called power angle. Max power when $\delta = 90^\circ$.

Example 15.13: Synchronous Motor Calculation

  • Y-connected, 3-phase. Per-phase $V_L = 125\text{V}$. $X_s = 5\Omega$. Input power $P_{total} = 6\text{kW}$. Torque angle $\delta = 27^\circ$. Find pf.
  • Per-phase power $P_{phase} = 6000\text{W} / 3 = 2000\text{W}$.
  • From $P = (V_L V_g / X_s) \sin \delta$: $V_g = (P X_s) / (V_L \sin \delta) = (2000 \cdot 5) / (125 \cdot \sin 27^\circ) = 10000 / (125 \cdot 0.454) \approx 176\text{V}$.
  • From $V_L = jX_s I_a + V_g$ (assuming $R_a=0$), solve for $I_a$: $I_a = (V_L - V_g) / (jX_s)$. If $V_L = 125\angle 0^\circ$, then $V_g = 176\angle -27^\circ$ (lags $V_L$ by $\delta$). $I_a = (125\angle 0^\circ - 176\angle -27^\circ) / (5\angle 90^\circ)$. $125 - (176\cos(-27^\circ) + j176\sin(-27^\circ)) = 125 - (156.8 - j79.9) = -31.8 + j79.9 = 86.0\angle 111.7^\circ$. $I_a = 86.0\angle 111.7^\circ / 5\angle 90^\circ = 17.2\angle (111.7-90)^\circ = 17.2\angle 21.7^\circ \text{ A}$. (Text gets $22^\circ$).
  • Power factor: $\cos(21.7^\circ) \approx 0.929$ leading (since $I_a$ leads $V_L$).
  • Overexcited Motor: $V_g > V_L$ (often leading pf).
  • Underexcited Motor: $V_g < V_L$ (often lagging pf).
  • Power Factor Correction (Fig. 15.60): By varying field current (DC to rotor), $V_g$ changes.
    • Increasing field current -> increases $V_g$. For constant load (power), $V_g \sin \delta$ must be constant. So if $V_g$ increases, $\sin \delta$ (and $\delta$) must decrease.
    • This changes $I_a$ and its phase angle $\theta$. Can change pf from lagging to leading.
    • An unloaded synchronous motor used for pf correction is a synchronous condenser.
  • Synchronous Speed:
$$ n_s = \frac{120f}{p} \quad (Eq. 15.42) $$

Motor runs exactly at this speed or stops.

  • Pull-out Torque: Max torque before losing synchronism.
  • Stepper Motor (Fig. 15.61): Variation that rotates in discrete steps.
    • Rotor is permanent magnet. Stator has multiple poles with windings.
    • Applying current to specific stator poles in sequence positions rotor. E.g. $45^\circ$ steps.
    • Used for positioning.
  • Starting Synchronous Motors: No inherent starting torque (average torque is zero if rotor is stationary). Requires starting mechanism (e.g., damper/squirrel-cage windings on rotor to act like induction motor at start).

The Induction Motor (Pages 1014-1026)

Most common AC motor. No DC excitation needed for rotor. Operates by magnetic induction.

  • Stator (field) creates rotating magnetic flux (at $\omega_s$).
  • Rotor (armature) has conductors.
  • Squirrel-Cage Rotor (Fig. 15.62): Conducting bars short-circuited by end rings. No brushes/slip rings. Rugged.
  • Operation (Fig. 15.63):
    1. Rotating stator field cuts rotor conductors.
    2. EMF induced in rotor conductors (Faraday's Law).
    3. Since rotor circuit is closed, induced currents flow in rotor conductors.
    4. Rotor currents in stator magnetic field experience forces -> torque.
    5. Torque tends to make rotor follow rotating stator field.
  • Slip (s): Rotor speed $n$ must be less than synchronous speed $n_s$. If $n = n_s$, no relative motion, no induced EMF, no torque.
$$ s = \frac{(n_s - n)}{n_s} \quad (Eq. 15.43) $$
  • Motor speed
$$ n = (1 - s)n_s \quad (Eq. 15.44) $$
  • Typical full-load slip: 2-6% (0.02-0.06).
  • Slip Speed:
$$ n_s - n = s n_s \quad (Eq. 15.45) $$

Relative speed between stator field and rotor.

  • Frequency of Rotor EMF/Currents ($f_r$): Proportional to slip speed. $f_r = (p \cdot (s n_s)) / 120 = s \cdot (p n_s / 120) = s f_s$ (where $f_s$ is stator frequency).
  • Rotor Field Speed: Rotor currents at $f_r$ create a rotor magnetic field. This field rotates relative to rotor at $s n_s$. Absolute speed of rotor field = $(\text{speed of rotor}) + (\text{speed of rotor field rel. to rotor})$ $= n + s n_s = (1-s)n_s + s n_s = n_s$. Rotor field rotates at synchronous speed, same as stator field. Constant torque produced.
  • Damping Windings: Squirrel-cage windings on synchronous motor rotor act as induction motor windings for starting and damping oscillations.

Example 15.14: Induction Motor Parameters

  • 3-phase induction motor, speed $n = 864 \text{ rpm}$ at $f_s = 60 \text{ Hz}$.
  • $n_s = 120 f_s / p = 7200 / p$. Since $n < n_s$, try $p$ values. If $p=8$, $n_s = 7200/8 = 900 \text{ rpm}$. This is close above 864.
  • Slip $s = (n_s - n) / n_s = (900 - 864) / 900 = 36 / 900 = 0.04 = 4\%$.
  • Rotor frequency $f_r = s f_s = 0.04 \cdot 60 \text{ Hz} = 2.4 \text{ Hz}$.

An Induction-Motor Model (Per-Phase Equivalent Circuit)

Like a transformer with rotating secondary.

  • Rotor at Rest (s=1, "locked rotor"): Stator field induces EMF $V_2$ (at frequency $f_s$) in rotor. Rotor current
$$ I_2 = \frac{V_2}{(R_2 + jX_2)} \quad (Eq. 15.46) $$

($R_2$=rotor resistance, $X_2$=rotor reactance at $f_s$).

  • Rotor Running (slip s): Induced EMF is $sV_2$ (at frequency $sf_s$). Rotor reactance is $sX_2$. Rotor current
$$ I_2 = \frac{sV_2}{(R_2 + jsX_2)} = \frac{V_2}{((R_2/s) + jX_2)} \quad (Eq. 15.47) $$
  • Equivalent Rotor Circuit (Fig. 15.64): Voltage $V_2$ drives current $I_2$ through $jX_2$ and $R_2/s$. The term $R_2/s$ can be split: $R_2/s = R_2 + R_2(1-s)/s$.
    • $R_2$: Actual rotor resistance (power loss $I_2^2 R_2$ as heat).
    • $R_r = R_2(1-s)/s$: Fictitious resistance representing mechanical power developed. $P_{mech\_per\_phase} = I_2^2 \cdot R_2(1-s)/s$.
  • Complete Equivalent Circuit (Fig. 15.65): Refer rotor quantities to stator (like transformer).
    • $R_1, X_1$: Stator resistance and leakage reactance.
    • Ideal transformer $1:N$ (N is effective turns ratio).
    • Reflected rotor impedance: $(R_2/s)/N^2 + jX_2/N^2$.
    • Can be simplified by lumping reactances and referring rotor resistance (Fig. 15.68 is standard).
    • Text model results in Eq on page 1020: $I_2 = (V/N) / (R_1 + (R_2'/s) + jX)$ where $R_2' = R_2/N^2$ and $X = X_1 + X_2/N^2$. (V is stator phase voltage).
    • Power converted to mechanical: $P_{conv} = I_2^2 \cdot R_2'(1-s)/s$ per phase. (Using the current $I_2$ from the simplified referred circuit, and $R_2'$ referred rotor resistance).

Torque-Speed Characteristics (Fig. 15.66)

  • Mechanical power $P_{mech} = T\omega_m$ ($\omega_m$ is mechanical speed of rotor in rad/s).
  • $\omega_m = (1-s)\omega_s$ (where $\omega_s$ is synchronous angular speed).
  • Torque (3-phase total): $T = (3 \cdot P_{mech\_per\_phase}) / \omega_m = (3 \cdot I_2^2 \cdot R_2'(1-s)/s) / ((1-s)\omega_s)$. $T = (3 \cdot I_2^2 \cdot R_2'/s) / \omega_s$. Substitute $I_2 = V / \sqrt{((R_1 + R_2'/s)^2 + X^2)}$ (where $V$ is phase voltage).
$$ T = \frac{3V^2 (R_2'/s)}{\omega_s [(R_1 + R_2'/s)^2 + X^2]} \quad (Eq. 15.51) $$
  • Normal Operation (small s, 2-6%): $R_2'/s$ is large. $R_1, X$ are small. $T \approx (3V^2s) / (\omega_s R_2')$. Torque approx. proportional to slip. Constant speed machine.
  • Slow Speeds (s $\approx$ 1, starting): $R_1 + R_2'/s$ small compared to $X$. $T \approx (3V^2 R_2') / (\omega_s X^2s)$. Starting torque (s=1): $T_{start} \approx (3V^2 R_2') / (\omega_s X^2)$.
  • Maximum (Breakdown) Torque ($T_m$): Differentiate $T$ wrt $s$, set to zero. Occurs at slip
$$ s_{maxT} = \frac{R_2'}{\sqrt{R_1^2 + X^2}} \quad (Eq. 15.52) $$
$$ T_m = \frac{3V^2}{2\\omega_s [R_1 + \sqrt{R_1^2 + X^2}]} \quad (Eq. 15.53) $$

Independent of $R_2'$.

Example 15.15: Induction Motor Torques

  • 3-phase, 120V/phase, $n = 1152 \text{ rpm}$. 60Hz, 6-pole. $R_1 = 0.25\Omega$, $R_2' = 0.15\Omega$ (referred), $X = 0.5\Omega$.
  • $n_s = 120 \cdot 60/6 = 1200 \text{ rpm}$. $\omega_s = n_s \cdot 2\pi/60 = 1200 \cdot 2\pi/60 = 40\pi \text{ rad/s}$.
  • Rated slip $s = (1200 - 1152) / 1200 = 48/1200 = 0.04 = 4\%$.
  • Rated Torque (Eq. 15.51): $T_r = (3 \cdot 120^2 \cdot (0.15/0.04)) / (40\pi \cdot [(0.25 + 0.15/0.04)^2 + 0.5^2])$ $R_2'/s = 0.15/0.04 = 3.75$. $R_1 + R_2'/s = 0.25 + 3.75 = 4$. $T_r = (3 \cdot 120^2 \cdot 3.75) / (40\pi \cdot [4^2 + 0.5^2]) = 162000 / (40\pi \cdot [16 + 0.25]) = 162000 / (40\pi \cdot 16.25) \approx 79.3 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}$.
  • Starting Torque (s=1): $R_2'/1 = 0.15$. $R_1 + R_2'/1 = 0.25 + 0.15 = 0.4$. $T_{st} = (3 \cdot 120^2 \cdot 0.15) / (40\pi \cdot [0.4^2 + 0.5^2]) = 6480 / (40\pi \cdot [0.16 + 0.25]) = 6480 / (40\pi \cdot 0.41) \approx 125.7 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}$.
  • Max Torque (Eq. 15.53): $T_m = (3 \cdot 120^2) / (2 \cdot 40\pi \cdot [0.25 + \sqrt{(0.25^2 + 0.5^2)}])$ $\sqrt{(0.0625 + 0.25)} = \sqrt{0.3125} \approx 0.559$. $T_m = 43200 / (80\pi \cdot [0.25 + 0.559]) = 43200 / (80\pi \cdot 0.809) \approx 43200 / 203.3 \approx 212 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}$. (Matches text).
  • Slip for max torque (Eq. 15.52): $s_{maxT} = 0.15 / \sqrt{(0.25^2 + 0.5^2)} = 0.15 / 0.559 \approx 0.268 = 26.8\%$. (Matches text).
  • Speed at max torque: $n = (1 - 0.268) \cdot 1200 = 0.732 \cdot 1200 = 878.4 \text{ rpm}$. (Matches text).
  • Rated mechanical output power: $P_{out} = T_r \cdot \omega_m = T_r \cdot (1-s)\omega_s = 79.3 \cdot (1-0.04) \cdot 40\pi = 79.3 \cdot 0.96 \cdot 40\pi \approx 9570 \text{ W}$. $9570 \text{ W} / 746 \text{ W/hp} \approx 12.8 \text{ hp}$. (Matches text).
  • Wound-Rotor Motor: Rotor has windings similar to stator, connected to slip rings. External resistances can be added in series with rotor coils.
    • Increasing $R_2$ (and $R_2'$) increases slip for given torque (speed control).
    • Increases starting torque (shifts $s_{maxT}$ point, see Fig 15.66 curve shape). Max torque value is independent of $R_2'$.
    • More expensive.

Single-Phase Induction Motors (Pages 1024-1026)

Common for low power ( < 1 hp). Stator has single-phase winding.

  • Single-phase stator current $i = I \cos \omega t$ produces a pulsating flux $\Phi = kI \cos \omega t$.
  • This can be decomposed into two counter-rotating fields of equal magnitude ($(1/2)kI$).
  • Net torque is pulsating. Average torque-speed curve similar to 3-phase once started.
  • No inherent starting torque. Requires starting mechanism.
  1. Shaded-Pole Motor (Fig. 15.67):
    • Stator has salient poles. A "shading coil" (short-circuited copper loop) around part of each pole.
    • Currents induced in shading coil delay flux in shaded part.
    • Creates a small, weak rotating field component -> low starting torque. Direction is from unshaded to shaded part.
  2. Split-Phase Motor (Fig. 15.68):
    • Two stator windings: Main winding and Auxiliary (starting) winding. Physically displaced.
    • Auxiliary winding designed to have different R/X ratio than main (e.g., higher R/X).
    • Currents in two windings are phase-shifted -> produces rotating field component -> starting torque.
    • Centrifugal Switch: Disconnects auxiliary winding when motor nears operating speed.
  3. Capacitor Motor (Fig. 15.69): Type of split-phase motor.
    • Capacitor in series with auxiliary winding to create larger phase shift between main and auxiliary currents.
    • Better starting torque.
    • Capacitor-Start Motor: Centrifugal switch disconnects aux winding + capacitor.
    • Capacitor-Run Motor (Permanent-Split Capacitor, PSC): Capacitor and aux winding remain connected. Improves running pf and efficiency. Capacitor value is a compromise between start/run.
    • Capacitor-Start, Capacitor-Run Motor: Two capacitors. One for starting (larger, with aux winding, switched out). One for running (smaller, remains with aux winding). Best performance, higher cost.

Summary (Pages 1026-1027)

Key points from the chapter:

  1. Conductor (len $l$, vel $u$) in $B$ field: $emf = Blu$.
  2. Conductor (len $l$, current $i$) in $B$ field: $force = Bli$.
  3. Mechanical circuits analogous to electric circuits.
  4. Dynamic transducers (moving-coil): Loudspeakers, mics, phonograph pickups.
  5. Electromagnet (moving-iron): Relays, solenoid plungers.
  6. d'Arsonval movement: Rotational, for DC ammeters, voltmeters, ohmmeters.
  7. Electrodynamometer: Rotational, for AC measurements (esp. voltmeters, wattmeters).
  8. Generator: Mech -> Elec. Motor: Elec -> Mech.
  9. Alternator (AC gen): Slip rings. DC gen: Split-ring commutator.
  10. DC gen: Field on stator, armature on rotor. Alternator: Field on rotor, armature on stator (usually).
  11. Rotating machine voltage: Displayed by magnetization curve.
  12. DC machine: Separately or self-excited (shunt, series, compound).
  13. DC gen external characteristic: Load voltage vs. load current.
  14. Synchronous machines: Salient-pole (low speed) or cylindrical (high speed) rotors.
  15. Three-phase machines: Analyzed per-phase.
  16. Synchronous speed: Depends on frequency and number of poles.
  17. DC motor speed: Easily controlled.
  18. DC motor speed-torque characteristic.
  19. DC motor speed regulation: No-load to full-load speed change.
  20. DC motor starting: Requires starting box (series resistance).
  21. Rotating stator field: Employed by AC motors.
  22. Synchronous motor: Constant speed. Used for power-factor correction.
  23. Induction motor: Transformer with fixed primary, rotating secondary.
  24. Squirrel-cage motor: Simplest induction motor type.
  25. Induction motor: Speed slightly less than synchronous. Essentially constant speed for given load.
  26. Slip: Measure of speed difference $(n_s - n)/n_s$.
  27. Induction motor torque-speed curve.
  28. Wound-rotor motor: Speed control, higher starting torque, higher cost.
  29. Single-phase induction motors: Similar to 3-phase squirrel-cage, low power.
  30. Single-phase starting: Shaded-pole, split-phase, capacitor motors.
